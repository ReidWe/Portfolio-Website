Superheterodyne AM/FM Radio

Overview

This project involved the complete assembly and analysis of a superheterodyne AM/FM radio receiver. The superhet architecture — the dominant receiver topology since the 1930s — works by converting incoming RF signals to a fixed intermediate frequency (IF) using a local oscillator and mixer stage, which allows for high selectivity and sensitivity regardless of the tuned station.

Every component was hand-soldered to the PCB, and the build process required careful attention to grounding, lead dress, and solder joint quality to avoid oscillation and noise issues. The project culminated in a comprehensive IEEE-format technical report covering the theory of operation, assembly process, troubleshooting steps, and measured performance.

Signal Chain

The receiver follows the classic superhet signal chain: incoming RF is amplified, mixed with a local oscillator to produce an intermediate frequency, filtered and amplified at IF, demodulated by the detector, and finally driven through an audio amplifier to the speaker.

Assembly & Soldering

The entire radio was assembled by hand on a through-hole PCB. This involved soldering resistors, capacitors, inductors, diodes, transistors, and IC packages. Particular care was taken with the RF and IF sections where poor solder joints or excess lead length can introduce parasitic capacitance and cause unwanted oscillation or signal loss.

Troubleshooting was a significant part of the process — cold solder joints, bridged pads, and component orientation errors all had to be identified and corrected. Each stage was tested incrementally before moving to the next, following a methodical signal-chain verification approach.
